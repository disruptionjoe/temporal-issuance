# E016: Issuance Precision Pass

## Purpose

Roadmap Phase 2B requires: a minimal definition of issuance and at least one discriminator
separating it from stochastic update, information flow, causal/event growth, and thermodynamic
entropy. Then the five-way toy model from `NORTH-STAR.md`. Kill condition: if no discriminator
survives, treat issuance as a relabeling candidate and weaken `TI-C019`.

## What Issuance Is Required To Do

From `HYPOTHESIS.md` (vNext) and `NORTH-STAR.md`:

- Issuance is the continual introduction of new possibility, capacity, or opportunity into the
  shared process.
- Without it, observers only rearrange what already exists; novelty eventually runs down; the
  future becomes increasingly determined by inherited state.
- With it, new possibility enters the shared process; observers are not the sole source of what
  reality becomes; adaptive search remains alive.

This gives us two functional requirements:

**FR-1 (type-novelty):** Issuance must produce states that are not instances of any prior
admissible type — not just new draws from an existing distribution.

**FR-2 (non-determination):** The new states must not be fully determined by any fixed rule
applied to prior state — otherwise issuance collapses into a deterministic or stochastic
closed-update rule.

## Definition Attempts And Why The Earlier Ones Fail

**D1: State-space expansion.** Issuance is the addition of new states that were not representable
in any prior configuration.

*Fails the meta-distribution objection.* If new states come from a meta-level ambient space, the
true state space was always that ambient space. D1 may describe epistemic discovery of a
pre-existing larger space, not ontological addition of new possibilities.

**D2: Non-predictability.** Issuance is a process whose outputs cannot be predicted by any
function of prior state and any fixed rule.

*Does not satisfy FR-1.* Pure randomness (stochastic update with a fixed distribution) is also
non-predictable in this sense. D2 does not separate issuance from stochastic update.

**D3: Language extension.** Issuance is a process whose outputs require extending the
description language and cannot be generated by any computable function from prior state.

*Too strong.* The computability condition would make issuance transcend physical plausibility.
Physical systems are generally believed to be at most Turing-computable. The language-extension
condition risks infinite regress.

## Minimal Working Definition

**D4:** A process exhibits issuance if and only if:

> (i) it produces states that are not instances of any type admissible before that production
> event, and (ii) that production is not determined by any prior state together with any fixed
> transition rule over the existing type-schema.

More compactly:

> Issuance is the source-side introduction of type-novel states that are not rule-generated
> from prior state.

Note what D4 does not say: it does not say the new states come from nowhere, and it does not
say they are causally unconnected to anything. It says only that they are type-novel and
not rule-generated within the existing schema. This is deliberately minimal: the task is to
name what would need to be true, not to explain how it happens.

## Discriminator Test

Testing D4 against each named competitor.

### Stochastic update (SU)

SU selects states from a fixed probability distribution over a fixed state space `S`. Each draw
is a new event but is an instance of an existing type. D4 condition (i) is violated: no
type-novel states are produced. **D4 excludes SU. Discriminator holds.**

Pressure point: a type-generating distribution (a distribution over type-schemas, not just
instances) satisfies D4 at the object level. But this is then just SU at the meta-level —
introducing a meta-stochastic system with a fixed distribution over types. The regress is live
and flagged as Pressure Point PP-1.

### Information flow (IF)

IF redistributes existing information between subsystems. Total information is conserved or
measurable by entropy. No new admissible types are produced. D4 condition (i) is violated.
**D4 excludes IF. Discriminator holds.**

No pressure point here beyond the standard one that all closed-system update can be framed as
information redistribution.

### Causal/event growth (CSG)

Standard sequential growth models (e.g., Rideout-Sorkin) add events to a causal set. All
events are of the same type (point events in a partial order). The growth rule is a fixed
transition probability over the current causal set. D4 condition (i) is violated for standard
CSG: no new event-types are produced, only new event-instances. **D4 excludes standard CSG.
Discriminator holds.**

Pressure point: quantum causal-set models may involve superpositions of causal sets with
different event-type labelings. Whether this counts as type-novelty under D4 depends on
whether the quantum superposition state was already admissible in the prior Hilbert space. If
it was, D4(i) is still violated (new instance, not new type). If the Hilbert space itself
grows, the case becomes more complex. Flagged as Pressure Point PP-2.

### Thermodynamic entropy (TE)

TE measures the distribution of a system over a fixed phase space (or fixed Hilbert space in
quantum stat-mech). Entropy increase is movement toward higher-probability macrostates within
the existing state space. No new types are produced. D4 condition (i) is violated.
**D4 excludes TE. Discriminator holds.**

## Summary Discriminator Table

| Competitor | Satisfies D4(i) type-novelty? | Satisfies D4(ii) non-determination? | Excluded by D4? |
|------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Stochastic update | No (instances of old types) | No | Yes |
| Information flow | No | N/A | Yes |
| Standard CSG | No (instances of old event-type) | No | Yes |
| Thermodynamic entropy | No | N/A | Yes |
| Issuance (D4) | Yes (by definition) | Yes (by definition) | No |

D4 discriminates issuance from all four named competitors in their classical forms.

## Five-Way Toy Model

Using a concrete mini-system. Let states be labeled elements. Observers hold records and
reconstruct temporal order from those records.

### Case 1: Closed Observer Update

State space: `S = {A, B, C}` (three fixed types)  
Transition: deterministic `T: A→B, B→C, C→A`  
Observer record: `[A, B, C, A, B, C, ...]`  
Temporal reconstruction: observer orders events by record position.

What this fails to explain: the universe can produce exactly three types of state. No new
structure is possible. "Novelty" is revisiting known states in a fixed cycle. Agency is
bounded by the fixed type-schema `{A, B, C}`. Adaptive search has no room to discover
genuinely new strategies because there are no new types to discover.

Formal signature: fixed `S`, deterministic `T`, orbit is periodic.

### Case 2: Stochastic Update

State space: `S = {A, B, C}` (same fixed types)  
Transition: stochastic, e.g., `T(A) = {B: 0.7, C: 0.3}`  
Observer record: `[A, B, B, C, A, A, B, ...]`

What changes vs Case 1: outcomes are unpredictable from finite observation. The trajectory
is non-periodic. But the state space is still `{A, B, C}` — only three types are admissible.
Genuine structural novelty is impossible. The stochasticity adds variance, not new types.

Formal signature: fixed `S`, stochastic `T` over `S`, `|S|` constant.

### Case 3: External Input

State space: `S = {A, B, C}` (fixed)  
External driver: `E = {0, 1}` (binary signal)  
Transition: `T: S × E → S`  
Observer sees: states in `S` driven by `E`.

What this adds vs Case 2: the system can be driven differently by the external signal. But
`|S|` is still fixed — only three types of state are possible. The external source is itself
a fixed-type system (binary signal); it introduces no new types into `S`. To gain new types
from an external source, the external source would need to exhibit issuance — which defers
the question.

Formal signature: fixed `S`, fixed `E`, transition `T` maps `S × E` into `S` with no type
extension.

### Case 4: Genuine Issuance

State space at time `t`: `Sₜ` (type-growing)  
`S₀ = {A, B, C}`  
`S₁ = {A, B, C, D}` where `D` is type-novel: not an instance of A, B, or C, and not
derivable from any rule applied to `S₀`.  
`S₂ = {A, B, C, D, E}` where `E` is type-novel.

Observer record: `[A, B, C, A, D, B, E, ...]`  
Observer can order the events they have recorded. But they cannot predict or represent `D` or
`E` from `S₀` alone — these are genuine additions to what was admissible.

What this enables: the universe can produce state-types that did not exist before. Adaptive
search can discover fundamentally new strategies, not just new combinations of existing ones.
Observers can encounter phenomena that require extending their conceptual schema.

Formal signature: `|Sₜ|` is non-decreasing, with `Sₜ \ Sₜ₋₁ ≠ ∅` for each issuance event,
and each element of `Sₜ \ Sₜ₋₁` satisfies D4.

What is not answered by the toy model: what supplies `D`? This is the open frontier. The
toy model posits that `D` enters; it does not explain the mechanism of entry. That is the
research burden for the next pass.

### Case 5: Observer-Side Temporal Reconstruction

This case is orthogonal to Cases 1–4. It operates over the observer's records regardless of
which source-side case underlies the process.

Observer holds records: `R = {(state, index) : state ∈ Sₜ_observed}`  
Observer imposes temporal order by comparing records using causal/logical/informational
consistency constraints.

Key result: the structure of the temporal reconstruction operation is the same whether the
underlying source is Case 1, 2, 3, or 4. The observer's temporal order is downstream of
the source process. The reconstruction layer (TI-C001) operates on records; whether those
records include type-novel states (Case 4) or not (Cases 1-3) is a separate question.

This validates the layer separation from `HYPOTHESIS.md` (vNext) and `NORTH-STAR.md`: the
reconstruction layer is a real and tractable target, but it is not the deepest claim.

### Five-Way Comparison

| Case | `|S|` grows? | Rule-generated? | Type-novel? | Temporal order? |
|------|-------------|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1. Closed update | No | Yes (deterministic) | No | Reconstructed from records |
| 2. Stochastic | No | Yes (distributional) | No | Reconstructed from records |
| 3. External input | No (S fixed) | Yes (conditional) | No | Reconstructed from records |
| 4. Issuance (D4) | Yes | No | Yes | Reconstructed from records |
| 5. Temporal recon. | N/A | N/A | N/A | Yes (observer-primary) |

The table shows that Cases 1–4 share the same observer-side reconstruction structure (Case 5)
but differ at the source level in exactly the way D4 specifies. The discriminator is real.

## Pressure Points

### PP-1: Meta-Distribution Regress

A system that randomly samples new types from a meta-distribution over type-schemas satisfies
D4 at the object level but is just stochastic update at the meta-level. Push up one level
and the discriminator reappears as a question about the meta-level system.

Assessment: this regress does not defeat D4 as a working definition, for the same reason
that "causation" is useful despite the causal-regress problem. D4 marks where type-novelty
enters; whether the meta-level is itself issuance-capable is a further question the program
must eventually address. This keeps D4 useful without making it foundational.

Impact on TI-C019: the claim that issuance is the deepest source-side primitive must be
treated carefully. D4 is a property that can be instantiated at any level of a hierarchical
system. The claim that the physical universe exhibits issuance at some fundamental level is
not blocked by PP-1 but is not answered by it either.

### PP-2: Quantum CSG

Quantum causal-set models may involve superpositions over causal sets with different
topological or labeling structure. If the Hilbert space of such a theory is fixed, then new
instances of existing types are being generated (Case 2 at the quantum level) and D4(i) is
not satisfied. But if the theory requires an expanding Hilbert space — as in some approaches
to quantum gravity with topology change — D4 might apply.

Assessment: this pressure point is live but deferred. It requires a specific quantum-gravity
model to adjudicate. Not a current blocker for D4 as a classical working definition.

## Claim Effects

**TI-C019: Survives this run. Status remains speculative.**

Reason for survival: D4 provides a minimal working definition of issuance with a surviving
discriminator against all four named competitors in classical form. The five-way toy model
demonstrates structural distinctness.

Reason not promoted: D4 is not yet formalized mathematically (no categorical definition,
no type-theoretic formulation). PP-1 (meta-distribution regress) is live. PP-2 (quantum CSG)
is deferred. The issuance-energy-cosmology bridge remains entirely unearned.

**TI-C001: No change.** The reconstruction layer is confirmed downstream of the source-side
cases. The five-way toy model makes the layer separation concrete.

## What Was Not Absorbed

D4 survived all four named competitors. The closest absorption risk was CSG, and it did not
absorb D4 for standard classical causal-set growth because standard CSG adds event-instances
of a fixed type, not event-types. The distinction holds.

## What Was Not Answered

1. What is the categorical formulation of D4? What are morphisms between issuance-capable
   systems? What is the right functor from a category of such systems to known mathematical
   structures?
2. How does D4 connect to the physical picture? What physical process would constitute
   state-space expansion in the sense of D4, at the level of quantum field theory or beyond?
3. Can the meta-distribution regress (PP-1) be blocked at any level, or does it always apply?
   Is there a sense in which issuance at the base level is genuinely different from SU at a
   meta-level?

## Next Targets

**Primary — Categorical formalization of D4:**

Define the category `IssSys` of issuance-capable systems (objects: type-growing state systems
satisfying D4; morphisms: maps that preserve the issuance property). Define the category
`CloSys` of closed systems (objects: fixed-schema state systems; morphisms: rule-preserving
maps). Prove that there is no fully faithful functor from `IssSys` to `CloSys` — i.e., that
issuance cannot be embedded into a closed system category without information loss. This would
give D4 a precise categorical witness.

**Secondary — D4 and the Čech/sheaf fixture:**

TI-C017 asks whether `C`-typed admissibility independently determines which Čech cocycles are
allowed. Under D4, if typed admissibility generates new admissible cocycle classes (not
determined by the base type-schema), that would be a first concrete instance of issuance at
the reconstruction layer. The Čech/sheaf fixture can serve double duty: testing TI-C017 and
testing whether type-novel structure appears in the admissibility-constrained setting.

**Tertiary — Meta-distribution self-reference:**

Build a formal toy model to test PP-1: does an object-level issuance system (D4 at level 0)
require D4 at level 1 (the meta-distribution level)? If D4 is self-referential in this way,
it may be a genuine primitive rather than a relabeling — because removing it from the
meta-level would require introducing it somewhere else. This would be evidence that D4 is not
merely a convenient relabeling but tracks something structurally irreducible.
