# Physical Candidates for Genuine State-Space Growth Under the Temporal-Issuance Gate

## Bottom line

After surveying the gate as stated in the repository and then checking the strongest current empirical and theoretical literatures the result is negative: **I do not see a current physical system that plausibly survives all six requirements at once**. The near-misses split into two camps. One camp gives very good **formation records** and clear **before/after traces** but is still absorbable as movement inside a fixed larger possibility space, especially CRISPR spacer acquisition and autocatalytic chemistry. The other camp gives mathematically clean or experimentally sharp **effective algebra changes** but does so through a fixed platform plus measurement schedule, low-energy effective description, reference-frame choice, or crossed-product construction, which is exactly the kind of absorber your gate is meant to defeat. The best *unexpected* candidate I found is **native CRISPR spacer acquisition**. ## What the repository gate actually demands

The repository’s physical adapter is stricter than ordinary “emergence” language. E057 and E110 require more than a new behavior, a new record, or a new effective model. A candidate must beat a fixed source null consisting of a fixed state space or Hilbert space, fixed observable algebra, fixed instrument family, fixed admissibility predicate, fixed richer source object, and fixed readout space. The positive witness must include at least one of: a non-isomorphic algebra growth witness, a genuinely new admissibility predicate, or construction-space growth. It must also show perturbation non-factorization, record preservation, and defeat gauge/name/contextuality absorbers. E145 sharpens the requirement further: a future winner would need an *internally specified* physical model that blocks singleton after-naming without importing a hidden global table. citeturn13view4turn14view0turn40view4turn18view0turn40view1turn40view3

That matters because many currently fashionable “novelty” claims are explicitly ruled out by construction. The repo has already parked horizon mode-crossing as fixed-source disclosure in the standard regime, and its generated-UV stress test concludes that finite-prefix freshness can survive while still being absorbed at the whole-family level by a singleton enumerator. That means a candidate cannot merely look diagonal or prefix-fresh; it has to resist the stronger completion-and-after-naming adversary as well. citeturn15view3turn15view4turn40view1turn40view2

## The best unexpected candidate is native CRISPR spacer acquisition

The most interesting *unexpected* candidate is **CRISPR adaptation in native microbial systems**, where the concrete transition is: a cell that previously lacks a particular spacer inserts a new spacer into its CRISPR array and thereby acquires a new guide sequence and recognition capability. This is not just first observation of a previously hidden item. It is a traceable physical formation event: Cas1–Cas2 integrates a new DNA fragment into the array; rates of acquisition can be measured experimentally; and natural time-series data in the human gut show that spacer-acquisition updates, while rare, are real and leave a durable genomic record. That gives you unusually strong support for criteria about **new formation** and **record preservation**. A natural perturbation protocol is also available: expose lineages before and after acquisition to cognate phage or plasmid and compare infection/defense outcomes while sequencing the arrays. citeturn31view9turn32view3turn31view11turn29search5turn29search19

On your gate, this candidate can plausibly score partial credit on criteria like “new formation” and “traceable event,” but it still does **not** convincingly satisfy algebra growth. The strongest adversarial redescription is straightforward: spacers are short DNA fragments inserted into a fixed molecular substrate by fixed adaptation machinery, so the observed novelty can be redescribed as occupancy of a pre-existing, even if astronomically large, sequence space under a fixed biochemical law. Put differently, the cell’s *actual current repertoire* grows, but the adversary can still say the underlying sequence alphabet, polymer chemistry, insertion mechanism, and possible-guide space were already there. That is precisely the “fixed larger source plus expanding access or occupancy” shape your absorber allows. The after-naming ceiling is especially hard here: once the new spacer exists, a retroactive singleton-style description can always say “my fixed model always contained that exact future spacer sequence.” I do not see a structural answer in the present literature that blocks that move from inside the biology itself. citeturn31view11turn29search22turn31view9turn32view3turn40view1

Even so, if you want the **best empirical near-miss with a real formation record**, this is the one I would advance first. It is better than most “open-ended novelty” stories because it gives you an endogenous update rule, a durable before/after trace, a straightforward intervention handle, and a nontrivial internal repertoire change. What it lacks is exactly what your gate makes load-bearing: a reason that the newly formed recognition operator is *not* just a newly occupied point in a fixed underlying source. citeturn31view9turn32view3turn31view11turn40view4

## Engineered quantum systems provide the sharpest witness obligations but are still absorbed

If the ranking criterion is sheer **sharpness of the witness obligation**, the cleanest modern examples are in **time-dynamic quantum error correction and monitored topological systems**. In the coding literature, Hastings and Haah’s “dynamically generated logical qubits” are explicit: viewed as a subsystem code, the code has **no logical qubits**, but a measurement pattern generates them. Recent experiments on dynamic surface codes then realize time-dependent implementations in which logical support moves in spacetime and error suppression is measured directly. On the face of it, this looks very close to “new admissible structure becomes available after a transition.” It gives a concrete before/after, a perturbation protocol, and preserved records. citeturn35view0turn31view3

But this same literature also contains the absorber. The logical structure is generated by a **measurement pattern** or a **time-dynamic circuit** on a fixed qubit substrate. That makes the strongest null model unusually strong: a fixed hardware Hilbert space, fixed gate and measurement family, and fixed periodic schedule already explain the new effective logical algebra. In your terms, that is almost the canonical example of “fixed richer source plus access schedule.” So although these systems are excellent for expressing the witness obligation precisely, they are poor candidates for actually beating it. They are the sharpest near-miss, not the winner. citeturn35view0turn31view3turn18view0

A closely related branch is **measurement-induced topology**. A recent photonic experiment reports that repeated measurements dynamically modify topological order, tailoring Zeno subspaces and driving a trivial Lieb lattice into a topological phase with unidirectional edge transport and insulating bulk signatures. The paper itself emphasizes that measurements tailor the Hilbert space and induce the transition. That gives you a real measured protocol for criteria about perturbation and preserved records. Yet it also makes the absorber obvious: the novelty is generated by an externally designed measurement/Floquet protocol, not by a source-side law that has somehow escaped fixed completion. The same problem afflicts recent Lindbladian anyon-condensation work. It is interesting that decoherence and dissipation can drive topological transitions, but the null model remains a fixed open-system law on a fixed substrate. citeturn36view0turn36view2turn36view3turn37view2

## Emergent gauge sectors and topological phase changes in quantum matter are impressive but still effective

The condensed-matter and simulator literature is full of systems that *sound* closer to your target: emergent gauge structure, confinement/deconfinement, anyon condensation, and new superselection sectors. A strong recent example is the observation of string breaking on a two-dimensional Rydberg quantum simulator, where a local U(1) symmetry emerges from the Rydberg blockade and the experiment maps a phase diagram and observes string-breaking dynamics. Another is the superconducting-circuit experiment showing emergent Z2 gauge invariance **despite the absence of gauge structure in the effective Hamiltonian**. Taken at face value, those are serious claims of newly emergent sectors or observables. citeturn41view1turn41view2turn41view3

For your gate, however, the strongest adversarial redescription remains undefeated. In both cases the “new” structure is explicitly an **effective** or **low-energy** gauge description realized inside a fixed programmable platform of atoms or qubits. The Rydberg case says the gauge theory is efficiently implemented on a chosen geometry; the superconducting case says gauge invariance emerges in a low-energy regime. That is powerful physics, but it is still not evidence that the microscopic source space itself grew in a way that cannot be redescribed by a fixed larger model plus a control schedule, energetic restriction, or effective coarse-graining. Put bluntly: these are outstanding examples of emergent description, not decisive examples of source-side state-space growth. citeturn41view1turn41view2turn41view3

The same verdict applies to the broader anyon-condensation line. Theoretical work now shows that decoherence and dissipation can dynamically produce anyon condensation and topological transitions in Kitaev-type systems, and related work on anyon-condensation webs studies cases where the number of effective anyon sectors increases. But all of that is still naturally read as a reorganization of superselection structure **within a fixed microscopic system**, not a proof that the source has escaped fixed-completion absorption. As a result, these systems may be good W1-style *effective* algebra-change models, but they remain weak on absorber defeat. citeturn37view2turn20search7turn23search17

## Quantum-gravity algebra changes are mathematically closest to algebra growth and physically farthest from winning

The cleanest formal candidate for your “algebra growth” language is the recent **crossed-product / gravitational-algebra** program. Chandrasekaran, Longo, Penington, and Witten describe the algebra of observables for a de Sitter static patch as a Type II1 von Neumann algebra, and parallel crossed-product work emphasizes that crossed products convert type III1 region algebras into type II∞ factors where entropy becomes well defined. From a purely algebraic point of view, this is exactly the sort of thing that looks like a change in the observable algebra itself rather than a widening window on a fixed finite-dimensional state space. citeturn31view6turn39academia16turn39search1turn39academia17

But once one asks your more hostile question — *is this source-side temporal growth rather than bookkeeping?* — the answer seems to flip. The crossed-product construction attaches extra structure through modular flow and observer/reference-frame data, and recent work explicitly analyzes the observer-dependence of the resulting gravitational entropy. That makes the strongest null model extremely powerful: the “algebra change” may be a change of description induced by modular dynamics or quantum reference frames, not a formation event in which the source itself physically manufactures a genuinely unformable new observable class. There is also no clear experimental protocol today that would satisfy your perturbation-non-factorization criterion rather than merely instantiate a new mathematical package for the same region. By your standards, this is therefore a beautiful *operator-algebraic near-miss* and a poor empirical candidate. citeturn39academia16turn39academia17turn39academia18turn31view6

This conclusion lines up with the repository’s own handling of cosmological horizon mode crossing. E139 treats standard horizon crossing as fixed-source disclosure and says that even self-generating variants collapse back to the class-relative OnlineIssuance residue unless they can beat the singleton ceiling. So the repo’s own strongest cosmological swing already points the right way: current algebraic gravity work is highly relevant vocabulary, but it is not yet evidence of physical source issuance that defeats absorber-class completions. citeturn15view3turn15view4turn14view1

## Chemical self-construction and ribozyme networks are the strongest W2 and W3 stories, but not W1

In chemistry, the strongest live candidates are **self-replicator catalysis** and **self-reproducing ribozyme networks**. Recent systems-chemistry work reports a synthetic self-replicator that catalyzes **bond-forming** acyl hydrazone reactions, explicitly increasing molecular complexity and diversity. On the origin-of-life side, recent ribozyme work combines generative models with high-throughput sequencing and estimates an enormous space of self-reproducing ribozymes, far beyond what older local mutational scans reached. These are good candidates if the emphasis is on “new formation,” “construction-space growth,” and “record preservation,” because the system constructs new catalytic species or functions and leaves clear molecular traces. citeturn34view0turn34view1turn34view4

Yet these are still not winners under your gate. The self-replicator paper itself is framed around chosen substrates and a defined assay, and the ribozyme paper explicitly discusses diversification around a reference ribozyme within a vast but fixed sequence space. That means the strongest adversarial redescription is again the familiar one: fixed alphabet, fixed monomer chemistry, fixed substrate menu, fixed physical law, and movement into a previously unoccupied but pre-existing possibility space. These systems probably give the **best chemistry-side W2/W3 intuition** now available, but not a compelling W1 defeat of fixed-source completion. And I do not see an internal mechanism in the present papers that blocks the after-naming ceiling: once the catalytic novelty is realized, a global lookup-table adversary can still claim it was always part of the larger chemical possibility space. citeturn34view0turn34view4turn40view1

## What a future winner would have to look like

A future winner would need to combine the best features of the near-miss classes while avoiding all of their standard absorbers. Concretely, it would have to look like a system in which a stage-n source really does have a restricted internal formation grammar, then a traceable event creates a stage-(n+1) observable or instrument type that was not formable at stage n, and interventions on that new type produce outcomes that **cannot** be reproduced by any fixed completed source plus widening access, decoherence schedule, low-energy restriction, or external measurement protocol. Just as important, the system would need an *internal anti-after-naming principle* — some physical analogue of a productive family or endogenous code/law discipline — so that retroactively naming the realized novelty is not an honest absorption but a change of model class. That is almost exactly the future-work burden E145 now states explicitly. citeturn40view1turn40view3turn14view0turn18view0

So the honest research verdict is this: **no current physics supplies a viable candidate that clearly passes your gate**. The closest empirical candidates are **CRISPR spacer acquisition** and **autocatalytic/ribozyme chemistry**, because they really do supply endogenous formation events with durable traces. The closest formal/algebraic candidates are **crossed-product gravitational algebras** and **monitored/topological quantum systems**, because they really do provide strong language of algebra change. But today the first group loses on fixed-completion absorption, and the second group loses on observer/schedule/bookkeeping absorption.
